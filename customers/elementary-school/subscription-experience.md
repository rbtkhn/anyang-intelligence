# Subscription Experience

This document defines what the **monthly continuity subscription** should feel like and produce in the Elementary School lane.

The goal is to turn the continuity offer into a real operating service instead of a vague promise.

## Purpose

The monthly subscription exists to help a parent keep the Student Operating System alive after the first 30-day plan.

It should provide continuity, interpretation, and steady parent support around the student's real education environment, whether that is public school, homeschool, or a hybrid, without replacing parent authority or creating an unsustainable support burden.

## Experience Shape

The continuity experience should feel like:

- a parent-guided operating rhythm
- a place to bring observations and questions
- a lightweight but recurring layer of interpretation
- a way to keep plans, reading choices, and portfolio evidence from going stale

The subscription is not meant to feel like emergency coverage, unlimited tutoring, or always-on household management.

## Monthly Subscription Includes

The monthly continuity subscription should include these recurring components:

- Access to the parent chat group.
- One premium newsletter rhythm.
- One personalized consultation rhythm.
- Early access to new upgrades and features when appropriate.
- A monthly recommendation refresh based on parent observations and learner signals.

## Chat Group

The chat group is the lightest-weight continuity layer.

Its job is to let parents:

- ask bounded planning questions
- share observation patterns
- compare friction and success patterns
- receive general guidance that still preserves parent authority

The chat group should not function as:

- crisis support
- diagnosis support
- legal/accountability authority
- a substitute for teacher, tutor, clinician, or specialist judgment
- an unlimited direct-message backlog

## Premium Newsletter

The premium newsletter should give parents a recurring layer of structured guidance.

Good newsletter content includes:

- seasonal planning ideas
- reading-basket refresh ideas
- portfolio prompts
- household-fit planning patterns
- observation cues to notice in a child
- examples of how to turn friction into next-step adjustments
- explanations of new Student Operating System upgrades

The newsletter should help parents get value even in weeks when they do not ask for direct help.

## Personalized Consultation

Personalized consultation is the higher-touch part of the subscription.

It should be defined narrowly enough to stay sustainable.

Good consultation work includes:

- reviewing parent observations
- helping interpret reading-basket patterns
- suggesting next-month adjustments
- helping simplify an overloaded plan
- helping adapt the system to household constraints
- helping decide what belongs in the portfolio
- helping identify when a concern should stay in ordinary planning versus when outside qualified support should be considered

Personalized consultation should not mean:

- unlimited on-demand access
- same-day guaranteed responses
- emergency decision coverage
- direct child-facing authority
- diagnosis, treatment, legal, or compliance advice

## Cadence

The subscription should run on a simple recurring rhythm:

- Ongoing chat-group access for bounded questions and shared learning.
- One premium newsletter each month.
- One personalized consultation touchpoint each month.
- One monthly recommendation refresh when enough observation signal exists.

If support load grows, the first fix should be tightening cadence promises, not quietly expanding labor.

## Response Expectation

The continuity offer should set calm, bounded expectations.

It is better to promise a humane review rhythm than to imply instant availability.

Recommended posture:

- questions are gathered and answered in a reasonable recurring rhythm
- higher-stakes issues return to parent judgment and qualified human support
- not every observation needs an immediate response to create value

## Monthly Recommendation Refresh

Each subscription month should ideally produce a visible next-step layer.

That refresh may include:

- reading-basket adjustments
- screen-time or app-use adjustments
- daily rhythm revisions
- portfolio capture suggestions
- project or enrichment recommendations
- watch-item reframing
- next-month parent review questions

This keeps the subscription tied to real operating improvement rather than ambient encouragement alone.

## Early Access To Upgrades

Early access should mean families can try improved tools before they are part of the normal lane.

Examples may include:

- new review templates
- improved parent prompts
- better observation surfaces
- refined reading-basket recommendation logic
- upgraded portfolio or monthly-review structures

Early access should never bypass safety, approval, or parent-authority guardrails.

## Operator Guardrails

Operators should preserve these subscription boundaries:

- Keep the service parent-facing and parent-led.
- Keep child-facing decisions behind explicit parent approval.
- Keep high-stakes concerns routed back to the appropriate human or professional support.
- Do not imply unlimited access.
- Do not let chat convenience erode privacy or evidence discipline.
- Do not confuse warm community support with licensed or formal educational authority.

## Success Signals

The subscription is working when:

- parents remain engaged after the first 30-day plan
- monthly questions become clearer, not more chaotic
- reading-basket, portfolio, and household-fit signals actually influence next recommendations
- parents report lower planning friction
- the support load remains sustainable for the operator

## Failure Signals

The subscription needs redesign when:

- parents expect unlimited custom planning
- the chat group becomes a hidden direct-support queue
- the newsletter becomes generic and skippable
- consultation work sprawls beyond the promised boundary
- families cannot tell what is free versus what belongs to continuity
