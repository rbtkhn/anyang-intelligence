# Reading Basket

The reading basket is the first physical literacy tool in the Student Operating System Startup Bundle.

Its job is to make reading visible, inviting, and easy to start.

## Purpose

The reading basket helps the parent:

- Keep daily reading low-friction.
- Notice what the child chooses voluntarily.
- Balance confidence, practice, challenge, curiosity, and read-aloud connection.
- Turn reading into portfolio evidence without making it feel like a test.
- Build a better next book list from real engagement.

## First Basket

Start with 5-10 books:

- 2 easy confidence books the child can read with low effort.
- 2 current-level books that fit the child's present reading ability.
- 1 stretch book that may need parent support.
- 1 interest-led nonfiction book.
- 1 parent read-aloud book.
- Optional: 1 graphic novel, poetry book, joke book, recipe book, field guide, or magazine tied to the child's interests.

The basket should feel abundant but not overwhelming.

## Selection Rules

Choose books by:

- Child interest first.
- Reading confidence.
- Format variety.
- Topic variety.
- Read-aloud usefulness.
- Short enough sessions to create wins.

Avoid:

- Turning every book into a lesson.
- Filling the basket only with books adults admire.
- Making stretch books dominate the basket.
- Treating unfinished books as failure.

## Daily Use

Default rhythm:

```text
child chooses
  -> read together or independently for 10 minutes
  -> parent notices one signal
  -> optional portfolio artifact
```

Parent observation prompts:

- What did the child choose first?
- Did the child reread, skim, ask for help, abandon, or linger?
- Which topic, character, image, or fact drew attention?
- Was the difficulty too easy, right-sized, or too hard?
- What should the next basket include?

## Portfolio Evidence

Save light evidence only when useful:

- A dated photo of the child reading.
- A copied favorite sentence.
- A drawing inspired by the book.
- A short parent note about interest or difficulty.
- A list of books tried, loved, abandoned, or requested.
- One question the child asked.

Do not turn the reading basket into paperwork. The evidence should help the parent see the learning arc.

## Weekly Review

Once per week, sort the basket into:

- Keep.
- Replace.
- Read aloud.
- Try again later.
- Find more like this.

Then choose one next reading action:

- Add easier confidence books.
- Add more nonfiction.
- Add more story-driven books.
- Add a read-aloud series.
- Add books connected to a current interest.
- Slow down and reread favorites.

## Success Criteria

The reading basket is working when:

- The child chooses books without heavy prompting.
- The parent learns what the child likes, avoids, and can handle.
- Reading becomes easier to start.
- The weekly review produces a better next basket.
- Portfolio evidence captures real engagement without making reading feel over-managed.

## Boundary

The reading basket is a starter literacy environment, not a full reading curriculum, reading diagnosis, or proof of grade-level mastery.

If reading frustration, avoidance, vision concerns, speech/language concerns, or persistent decoding difficulty appears, the parent should slow down and consider teacher, tutor, clinician, or specialist support.
