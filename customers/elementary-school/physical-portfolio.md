# Physical Portfolio

The physical portfolio is the place where the learner keeps work they are proud of and milestones worth remembering.

Its job is to make growth visible to the child and useful to the parent.

## Purpose

The physical portfolio helps:

- Honor the learner's pride and effort.
- Save milestone work before it disappears.
- Give the parent concrete evidence for weekly and monthly review.
- Show progress across reading, writing, math, art, projects, questions, and habits.
- Build the child's sense that learning creates a visible body of work.

## Setup

Use one simple container:

- Binder.
- Folder.
- Magazine file.
- Portfolio box.
- Accordion folder.

Add simple sections:

- Reading.
- Writing.
- Math.
- Art and projects.
- Questions and discoveries.
- Milestones.
- Parent notes.

Do not over-design the system. It should be easy enough to use on an ordinary day.

## What Goes In

Save work that fits one of these standards:

- The child is proud of it.
- It shows a new skill.
- It shows persistence through difficulty.
- It captures a meaningful question.
- It represents a milestone.
- It helps the parent understand the child's learning.

Examples:

- A favorite drawing.
- A writing sample.
- A math page the child worked hard on.
- A photo of a project.
- A book response.
- A question the child asked.
- A before/after sample.
- A parent note about a breakthrough.

## Child Choice Rule

The child should help choose what goes into the portfolio.

Ask:

- What are you proud of this week?
- What was hard but you kept trying?
- What do you want to remember?
- What should we show future-you?

The parent may also save milestone evidence, but the portfolio should not become only an adult assessment file.

## Weekly Use

Once per week:

- Choose 1-3 pieces to save.
- Date each item.
- Add a short parent note when helpful.
- Ask the child why the item matters.
- Notice one pattern for next week.

The weekly portfolio habit should take 10-15 minutes.

## Monthly Review

Once per month, review the portfolio and ask:

- What improved?
- What did the child become proud of?
- What topics keep appearing?
- What skills need more support?
- What should we do more of next month?
- What milestone should be celebrated?

The monthly review turns scattered student output into a learning arc.

## Milestone Log

Keep a simple milestone page:

```text
Date:
Milestone:
Evidence saved:
Parent note:
Child reflection:
Next support:
```

Milestones can be academic, creative, social, emotional, or habit-based.

Examples:

- Read a book independently.
- Finished a first story.
- Solved a hard math problem.
- Asked a deep question.
- Explained an idea clearly.
- Completed a project.
- Returned to something difficult.

## Boundary

The physical portfolio is not a gradebook, diagnosis, legal compliance file, or complete transcript by itself.

It is a parent-reviewed evidence and celebration system. If portfolio evidence raises concerns about reading, writing, attention, speech/language, math, behavior, health, or development, the parent should consider teacher, tutor, clinician, or specialist support.
