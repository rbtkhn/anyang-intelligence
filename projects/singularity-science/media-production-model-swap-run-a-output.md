## 1. Visual concept directions

### Direction A — The Layered City

Show one fictional city through four visual layers: a hand-drawn map, an archive box, a monument silhouette, and waveform lines representing oral history. Each layer appears incomplete alone and becomes legible when placed over the others.

### Direction B — The Moving Archive

Follow a fictional bundle of records moving through different hands: a mapmaker, a caretaker, a family storyteller, and a future student. Use recurring framing and changing textures to show memory as an active process rather than a fixed object.

### Direction C — The Two Maps

Place an official-looking fictional map beside a community-drawn map. The visual language gradually reveals that each map answers a different question about what matters, what is visible, and what has been left out.

## 2. Recommended direction

Recommend **The Two Maps**. It creates a clear visual argument without requiring real historical claims: memory is shaped by selection, perspective, and purpose. It also gives the producer a flexible structure for later inserting verified archival material, interviews, or location-specific examples.

## 3. Opening narration draft

“A city does not remember in only one way. It remembers through the places it marks, the stories people repeat, the records it preserves, and the details it leaves outside the frame. One map can show streets and boundaries. Another can show belonging, loss, work, or celebration. Together, they reveal that memory is not just stored in buildings or documents. It is made whenever a community decides what deserves to be carried forward.”
