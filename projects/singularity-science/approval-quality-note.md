# Approval Quality Note

This note extends the turn-usefulness framework by asking whether the human checkpoint was real.

A workflow can look efficient while still degrading governance if approval becomes symbolic, rushed, under-informed, or detached from the actual decision surface.

## Core Claim

Meaningful approval is part of the product.

A strong turn is not enough if:

- the approver does not hold the right authority
- the approver does not see the real risks
- the approver is shown only a polished final draft
- uncertainty is hidden at the moment of review
- approval is treated as blanket permission for future adjacent actions

## What A Good Approval Looks Like

A good approval checkpoint makes visible:

- who is deciding
- what authority they actually hold
- what they are approving
- what evidence they saw
- what remains uncertain
- what is still not approved
- what follow-up is still required

The approval should change the workflow state in a way a future operator can understand.

## Common Failure Modes

Weak or symbolic approval often looks like:

- a thumbs-up without review of the real risk
- approval of a draft that still hides provisional assumptions
- approval that is treated as permission for a broader use than was reviewed
- approval under deadline pressure with unresolved evidence gaps
- approval by someone adjacent to the authority rather than the actual authority

## Suggested Assessment Scale

Use this simple assessment in approval receipts:

- `strong`: the right authority reviewed the real decision, saw the key evidence, and the boundary of approval is clear
- `mixed`: real review happened, but some uncertainty, scope blur, or follow-up load remains
- `weak / symbolic`: the approval signal exists, but the real decision surface or authority boundary stayed blurry

## High-Value Receiving Lanes

Approval quality matters most where the cost of symbolic approval is high:

- Learning Core: parent authority, child-facing language, privacy, and support boundaries
- Grace Gems: owner approval over claims, pricing, customer promises, and storefront changes
- retired Non-Profit project: leadership or board authority over external claims, donor or grant commitments, restrictions, and stewardship

## Practical Use

Pair artifacts this way:

```text
turn receipt
  -> shows what the workflow turn absorbed and produced

approval receipt
  -> shows whether the human checkpoint was meaningful
```

Use both when a lane has a strong approval boundary and the workflow risks confusing draft quality with authority.

## Boundary

Do not use approval-quality language to pressure faster approvals or weaker oversight.

The purpose is to improve visibility, not to convert high-trust governance into rubber-stamp throughput.
