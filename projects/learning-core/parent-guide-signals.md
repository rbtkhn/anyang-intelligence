# Parent Guide Signals

This note lists parent-guide signals the Student Operating System can use to fit the plan to the real household.

These are not learner labels. They are parent-set budgets, boundaries, and operating realities that help the system adapt instead of forcing an idealized routine onto the family.

Use these signals during intake, readiness review, and plan drafting when the parent provides them.

## Core Idea

The parent should be able to set practical operating constraints, and the SOS should adjust to fit.

That includes signals like:

- how much screen time feels acceptable
- how much prep the parent can handle
- how much supervision is realistic
- how much evidence collection is tolerable
- what signs mean the child needs a pause

These signals help the plan match the actual home environment, not a generic student profile.

## Signal Set

### 1. Screen-Time Budget

Question:

- How much daily or weekly screen time should the learning plan fit inside?

Why it matters:

- Keeps app use inside parent-defined limits.
- Prevents screen-based supports from expanding by drift.

How the SOS should adapt:

- Fit all screen-based learning inside the parent budget.
- Treat Khan Academy Kids as part of that budget unless the parent says otherwise.

### 2. Parent Energy Budget

Question:

- On a low-energy day, what kind of learning day is still realistic?

Why it matters:

- The parent is the authority layer and often the implementation bottleneck.
- A plan that assumes full daily energy will fail under real household conditions.

How the SOS should adapt:

- Offer low-energy, standard, and fuller-day modes.
- Prefer simple repeatable moves on low-capacity days.

### 3. Prep Budget

Question:

- How much setup are you realistically willing to do before a learning block?

Why it matters:

- High-prep plans create friction and non-use.

How the SOS should adapt:

- Prefer low-prep activities when the budget is low.
- Reserve multi-step materials or setup-heavy activities for clearly approved days.

### 4. Cleanup Budget

Question:

- How much cleanup or reset feels manageable after activities?

Why it matters:

- Mess tolerance directly shapes what activities are actually sustainable.

How the SOS should adapt:

- Match activity choices to real cleanup tolerance.
- Avoid turning art, sensory, or project work into a household burden.

### 5. Supervision Budget

Question:

- Which activities need close parent presence, and which can happen with nearby oversight?

Why it matters:

- Not every plan element can assume full adult attention.

How the SOS should adapt:

- Reserve direct-instruction items for high-supervision windows.
- Put lighter independent or adjacent activities in lower-supervision windows.

### 6. Transition Tolerance

Question:

- Does your child do better with fewer longer blocks or more short switches?

Why it matters:

- Some children resist frequent switching; others need it.

How the SOS should adapt:

- Shape the rhythm around fewer transitions or more variety based on parent observation.

### 7. Frustration Threshold

Question:

- What signs tell you it is better to pause, pivot, or shorten an activity?

Why it matters:

- Parents often know the earliest useful signals before distress escalates.

How the SOS should adapt:

- Build pause-and-pivot rules into the plan.
- Avoid turning persistence into pressure.

### 8. Device-Role Boundary

Question:

- Should devices be support-only, travel-only, limited to certain times, or integrated into the normal rhythm?

Why it matters:

- Screen rules are often about role, not just duration.

How the SOS should adapt:

- Place device use only in the parent-approved role.
- Avoid treating screens as the default filler for rough days unless the parent wants that.

### 9. Novelty Budget

Question:

- How many new tools, routines, or activity types do you want introduced in a typical week?

Why it matters:

- Too much novelty can overwhelm both parent and child.

How the SOS should adapt:

- Limit new elements when the novelty budget is low.
- Reuse familiar structures more aggressively.

### 10. Out-Of-Home Budget

Question:

- How often are library trips, outside activities, field trips, or errands-based learning actually realistic?

Why it matters:

- Plans often over-assume mobility and spare time.

How the SOS should adapt:

- Keep out-of-home activities proportional to real household capacity.

### 11. Evidence Burden Budget

Question:

- How much documentation feels realistic before the portfolio starts feeling like paperwork?

Why it matters:

- The portfolio should preserve signal, not create admin drag.

How the SOS should adapt:

- Lower photo, note, and artifact expectations when the burden budget is low.
- Prefer a few strong pieces over constant capture.

### 12. Recovery Rule

Question:

- After a rough day, sick day, travel day, or overload day, what should the next day look like?

Why it matters:

- Families need recovery logic, not only ideal-day logic.

How the SOS should adapt:

- Build a restart pattern that protects confidence and continuity.

## Best First Signals

If the intake needs to stay light, the highest-value signals to ask first are:

- screen-time budget
- parent energy budget
- prep budget
- frustration threshold
- evidence burden budget

These give the strongest fit improvements with the least extra intake burden.

## Boundary Reminder

These signals guide planning. They do not replace:

- parent authority
- child safety judgment
- outside professional guidance when needed
- legal or accountability requirements

They are operating signals, not diagnoses or verdicts.
