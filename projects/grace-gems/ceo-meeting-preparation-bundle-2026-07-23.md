## Agenda

### 1. Current business challenges

- What is Grace Gems’ biggest constraint today: customer demand, production capacity, inventory, or decision-making bandwidth?
- Which storefront or product area deserves attention first?

### 2. Operational pain points

- Where do custom orders, customer claims, documentation, fulfillment, returns, or repairs create the most rework?
- Can the CEO walk through a recent example?

### 3. Customer experience

- Which customer promises are hardest to deliver consistently?
- Where is the greatest opportunity to build trust?

### 4. AI executive model discussion

- Would a reusable AI trust layer simplify operations or create additional maintenance work?
- What focused AI initiative could deliver measurable value within the current monthly support capacity?

### 5. Expected outcomes

By the end of the meeting, seek alignment on:

- one clear business priority;
- one focused first-review scope;
- relevant customer or operational evidence;
- decision-making roles and approval boundaries;
- success metrics;
- next steps, owner, and review timeline—or a decision to pause.

No external action, publication, pricing change, customer communication, or policy change is assumed or authorized by the meeting itself.

## Executive Assistant Brief

### Core posture

Lead with business understanding, not AI enthusiasm.

The working hypothesis is that Grace Gems’ challenge may be less about response speed and more about controlling the chain from:

```text
product information
  -> customer expectation
  -> custom specification
  -> fulfillment
  -> documentation
  -> customer trust
```

Present this as a hypothesis for the CEO to confirm, refine, or reject.

### Conversation flow

1. Identify the binding constraint.
2. Locate the operational leakage and rework.
3. Understand the highest-risk customer promises.
4. Test whether AI would reduce work or add burden.
5. Establish authority and privacy boundaries.
6. Narrow the work to one measurable first review.

### Closing question

> “Based on what we discussed, what would be the smallest useful first review—and what evidence would convince you that it helped?”

### Escalation triggers

Stop and clarify if the conversation moves toward:

- changing or publishing listings;
- product, material, authenticity, appraisal, or certificate claims;
- pricing or policy changes;
- customer communications;
- spending or advertising;
- private customer, order, cost, or supplier information;
- commitments on behalf of Grace Gems.

## Rehearsal Script

### Opening

> “Thank you for making time today. The goal is to understand where Grace Gems would benefit most from focused operating support—not to assume that AI is the answer. We want to leave with one clear priority, a bounded first review, and a way to measure whether it creates value.”

### Business challenge transition

> “Before discussing solutions, I’d like to understand where the business is most constrained right now: demand, production capacity, inventory, or decision-making bandwidth.”

Follow-up:

> “Which of those creates the greatest cost or frustration in a typical week?”

### Operational transition

> “We’re interested in the chain from product information to customer expectation, custom specification, fulfillment, and documentation. Where does that chain most often require rework?”

Follow-up:

> “Could you walk us through a recent example?”

### Trust transition

> “We’ve identified possible pressure around custom orders, product claims, certificates, shipping, returns, and repairs. Which of those is actually most important from your perspective?”

### AI transition

> “The question is not whether we can produce more AI-generated material. It is whether a focused AI trust layer could reduce repeated explanations, prevent unclear promises, or recover decision time without creating another maintenance burden.”

### Authority transition

> “What may we review or prepare, and what should always remain under your direct approval?”

Clarify who approves listings, pricing, claims, customer communications, refunds, policies, and spending.

### Closing

> “Let me summarize what I heard, and you can correct anything that is wrong.”

Summarize the priority, problem, evidence, scope, authority, metric, owner, and review date.

> “Would you like to proceed with that bounded review, refine it further, or pause?”

If asked to take immediate action:

> “I can capture that as a proposed next action. Before it becomes execution, we’ll confirm the scope, approval, and evidence required.”

## Evidence Request for the First Review

Request only information connected to the priority agreed during the meeting. This may include:

- the 5–10 listings or products most relevant to the review;
- recurring customer questions or support patterns, with private details removed;
- relevant product, material, certificate, appraisal, shipping, or return information;
- approximate pricing, cost, labor-time, or fulfillment context where relevant;
- current production capacity and lead-time constraints;
- the specific customer or operating problem the review should address;
- boundaries around what may be reviewed, drafted, or recommended;
- the outcome that would demonstrate useful progress.

Private customer, order, supplier, and financial records remain in the approved external location. Use only the information necessary for the agreed review.
