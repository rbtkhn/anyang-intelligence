## Review definition

- Business priority: preserve accurate shipping-origin promises and customer
  trust across the 13-store portfolio.
- Decision this review should support: determine whether the current
  United-States ship-from promise is consistently supported by operating
  evidence and what minimum verification control should preserve that accuracy.
- Selected workflow: the final customer-shipment leg for a maximum of 10 recent
  completed orders, selected by the owner to include both jewelry segments and
  each materially different fulfillment path.
- Why this scope now: the CEO confirmed that every order physically enters the
  carrier network in the United States and that storefront shipping settings
  have been updated accordingly.
- What remains outside scope: supplier origin, manufacturing origin, customs
  classification, historical portfolio-wide proof, shipping-rate changes,
  storefront edits, carrier changes, customer contact, and process automation.

## Evidence

- Evidence to review:
  - redacted order-to-shipment reference for each sampled order;
  - carrier name, first carrier-acceptance date, city, state, and country;
  - applicable storefront and fulfillment-path category;
  - public ship-from wording visible during the review.
- Source locations: owner-authorized private order or shipping system outside
  Git, plus the public storefront pages.
- Privacy or membrane limits: exclude customer names, addresses, messages,
  complete tracking numbers, payment details, supplier identities, and raw
  screenshots. Record only sanitized aggregate findings in Git.
- Known evidence gaps: evidence location and reviewer access are not yet
  confirmed; a bounded sample tests the operating control but cannot prove every
  historical shipment.
- Evidence interface: the Executive Assistant retrieves or reviews only the
  owner-authorized
  minimized evidence and returns a redacted evidence receipt. The Chief
  Executive analyzes only that returned evidence.

## Recommendation boundary

- Proposed analysis or deliverable: a sample-level origin-control table,
  exception record, and recommendation to retain, revise, or hold the current
  verification method.
- Actions permitted: the Executive Assistant may retrieve the approved redacted
  sample and
  return a minimized evidence receipt; the Chief Executive may compare that
  evidence with the public promise and prepare a recommendation.
- Actions not permitted: edit shipping settings or listings; contact customers,
  carriers, or suppliers; change packaging or routing; expose tracking or
  address data; infer manufacturing origin from shipping origin.
- Approval required before use: CEO approval before any listing, claim,
  customer communication, or fulfillment-control change.

## Measurement

- Primary value metric: percentage of sampled orders whose first customer-leg
  carrier acceptance occurred in the United States.
- Baseline: CEO-confirmed operational statement; record-level baseline not yet
  reviewed.
- Target or comparison: 100% of the bounded sample supports the current
  ship-from promise; any exception requires `Hold` and investigation before a
  recommendation to retain the promise.
- Quality, trust, rights, or burden guardrail: retain only the minimum redacted
  fields needed to establish carrier acceptance; do not conflate ship-from,
  supplier, or manufacturing origin.
- New burden introduced: selection and redaction of no more than 10 records,
  followed by one CEO decision.
- Net operating judgment: approved review question; evidence review held until
  the private source and reviewer access are explicitly authorized.

## Review and decision

- Planned completion date: not assigned.
- Review authority: CEO-approved bounded shipping-origin review.
- Continue if: the approved sample and first-acceptance evidence can be reviewed
  without exposing customer data.
- Revise if: the fulfillment paths cannot be represented within 10 records or
  carrier evidence uses materially different acceptance semantics.
- Stop or hold if: any first acceptance is outside the United States, evidence
  is missing or contradictory, or private data cannot be safely minimized.
- Next learning cycle: CEO decision on whether a lightweight origin-verification
  control should be designed.
