# Grace Gems Phase 2 Storefront First-Review

**Review date:** 2026-07-24  
**Brief:** [Phase 2 Storefront First-Review Brief](first-review-brief-2026-07-24.md)  
**Evidence boundary:** Public Etsy surfaces only  
**Status:** `review complete - CEO decision pending; implementation held`

## Executive finding

The most important public-evidence finding is not a weak storefront. It is a
portfolio-wide promise-control problem.

Across the shops, strong customer promises recur around natural or untreated
stones, handmade or handcrafted production, shipping origin, turnaround,
returns, continuous support, gift packaging, and custom work. Several promises
appear in different forms across listings and shops. In a few cases, statements
visible on the same public listing are difficult to reconcile, especially
shipping-from-US language beside a Hong Kong dispatch location.

This does not establish that the underlying products or operations are
deficient. It establishes that the public promise layer needs a controlled
source of truth before Grace Gems scales an independent website or expands
custom work.

## Observation labels

- **Observed:** visible on a public Etsy shop or listing surface reviewed on

- **Not observed:** not found in the bounded public surface reviewed; this does

## Most important Phase 2 insights

1. **Promise governance is the first scalable control.** The independent
   website should not simply copy Etsy listings. It needs an approved claim and
   policy source of truth.
2. **Custom work needs a common intake spine.** CrownJewelsCoUS's visible
   CAD/stone/approval/update sequence is a useful starting pattern, but the
   specification, change, payment, certification, timing, and return rules must
   be owner-approved.
3. **Shipping origin and responsibility require immediate verification.**
   Public US-shipping language and Hong Kong dispatch evidence coexist in more
   than one shop. This is a customer-expectation risk independent of actual
   delivery performance.
4. **Fine and affordable should not share one undifferentiated positioning
   model.** The fine segment needs controlled trust, documentation, and service
   signals; the affordable segment needs sharper assortment and value
   propositions without unsupported authenticity language.
5. **The portfolio is too broad for shop-by-shop improvisation.** Repeated
   packaging, service, return, material, and fulfillment promises should be
   defined once, with allowed segment or storefront variations.

## Recommended action

Run one CEO-approved **portfolio promise verification sprint** before making
website or listing changes.

The sprint should validate only these fields for each active storefront:

1. material and treatment terminology;
2. certificate or appraisal availability, price, issuer, and timing;
3. made-to-order/custom workflow and approval points;
4. actual production and dispatch origin;
5. processing, rush, carrier, and delivery commitments;
6. packaging standard;
7. cancellation, return, repair, and warranty rules;
8. accountable owner for each promise.

The first deliverable should be an internal source-of-truth table, not revised
customer copy. After CEO approval, one fine-jewelry and one affordable-jewelry
shop can be used as controlled pilots.
